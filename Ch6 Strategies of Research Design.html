Two Fundamental Orientations

Research design begins with a core orientation. Are you working with numbers to test a hypothesis, or with words and images to explore meaning? This choice shapes every decision you make.

Qualitative

  • Purpose: To understand and interpret social life from the participants' point of view.
  • Process: Non-linear and cyclical. The research path can change as new discoveries are made.
  • Logic: Primarily inductive. Theory emerges *from* the data.
  • Data: "Soft" data‚Äîwords, images, observations, symbols.
  • Language: Rich, descriptive language that captures context and meaning.

Quantitative

  • Purpose: To measure objective facts and test causal relationships between variables.
  • Process: Linear and systematic. Follows a fixed sequence of steps.
  • Logic: Primarily deductive. Data is used to test a pre-existing theory.
  • Data: "Hard" data‚Äînumbers from precise measurement.
  • Language: Precise technical language of variables and hypotheses.

Triangulation: The Power of Multiple Perspectives

Smart researchers don't rely on a single viewpoint. Triangulation is the practice of using multiple methods, theories, or observers to get a more accurate and complete picture of reality.

Key Features of Qualitative Design

Qualitative design is flexible and emergent, adapting to the rich details of social life.

Grounded Theory: Instead of starting with a theory, you build it from the "ground up" based on what you observe in the field.
The Importance of Context: Actions and events are understood within their specific social and historical context. The same behavior can have different meanings in different settings.
Focus on Cases & Process: Research often involves an in-depth study of a few cases to understand how a process unfolds over time, rather than a snapshot of many people.
Bricolage: The researcher acts like a creative tinkerer, using a diverse range of tools and methods to piece together a coherent understanding from the available evidence.

Key Features of Quantitative Design

Quantitative design is about precision, measurement, and testing clear, logical relationships.

Variables & Attributes

A variable is a concept that can take on two or more values (e.g., 'Gender'). An attribute is a specific value of a variable (e.g., 'Male' or 'Female').

Types of Variables

  • Independent Variable (IV): The cause. The factor you believe influences the outcome.
  • Dependent Variable (DV): The effect or outcome. This is what you measure.
  • Intervening Variable: A variable that comes between the IV and DV, helping to explain the relationship (IV ‚Üí Intervening ‚Üí DV).

The Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a testable statement about the relationship between two or more variables. It proposes a cause-and-effect connection that can be empirically verified.

  • Alternative Hypothesis (H‚ÇÅ): Your research prediction (e.g., "Higher education level causes lower prejudice").
  • Null Hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ): The logical opposite, stating there is *no relationship* (e.g., "There is no relationship between education level and prejudice"). Your goal is to disprove the null hypothesis.
